Every week, someone releases a new prompt playbook. Buy it for $97, and you get a collection of battle-tested prompts that will supposedly make you a better AI operator. There are McKinsey consultant prompts, executive coach prompts, expert negotiator prompts. The premise is simple: talk to AI the right way and it will produce better results.

This is complete nonsense. The real problem: judgment doesn’t transfer. You can’t download someone else’s judgment and paste it into a chat window.

**Here’s what actually separates people who get real results from AI from people who get fancier-sounding garbage: judgment. Everything else flows downstream from it.**

Think about what happens after AI gives you its first response. Every decision you make from that point forward is a judgment call.

-   Do you push back or accept it?
-   Do you execute what you got or ask a follow-up?
-   Do you recognize when the output is bullshit?

All of that is judgment. The prompt that got you the response is a rounding error compared to what you do with it.

The problem is that most people don’t think about AI collaboration this way. They’re operating with a broken mental model — treating AI like a better search engine, a magic box you ask questions and get answers from. They outsource the judgment entirely and wonder why nothing works.

But judgment isn’t a single skill you either have or don’t. **And here’s the uncomfortable truth:** **if you’ve been buying prompt playbooks, you’ve been training yourself to outsource the one thing that actually matters**. Every dollar you spent was a vote against your own capability. Judgment is a stack of meta-skills nested inside each other.

-   Decision-making under uncertainty.
-   Knowing which questions are worth asking.
-   Filtering information for what actually matters.
-   Recognizing when something is structurally wrong versus superficially broken.

Each layer requires judgment to operate, which means judgment is embedded in every part of the process — not just the final call.

You can’t outsource any of it to AI. Because AI doesn’t have judgment. It has pattern matching. Those are not the same thing.

## Every Decision You Make With AI Is a Judgment Call

Press enter or click to view image in full size

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*z1oFjHMSmMG3tMgCy5PeIA.png)

Most people think of judgment as the final decision — the moment you pick option A over option B. But that’s just the tip. The real work is everything underneath it.

Every decision exists in a field of uncertainty. The outcome of almost any decision you make is fundamentally unknowable in advance. Jump off a building without a parachute and the outcome is clear. The question isn’t how to eliminate uncertainty — it’s how to make good decisions inside it.

Annie Duke breaks it down this way: every decision has a probability of outcomes, not a single outcome. A bad outcome doesn’t mean it was a bad decision, and a good outcome doesn’t mean it was a good one. A poker player who shoves all-in on a bad hand and wins still made a bad decision — they just got lucky. Most people confuse the two and end up reinforcing the wrong behavior because the outcome happened to land in their favor.

So judgment starts with decision-making under uncertainty. But you need information to make decisions, which means you need to know what questions to ask. And you need judgment to know which questions are worth asking. And once you get answers, you need judgment to filter which information actually matters.

It’s recursive all the way down. Judgment isn’t a stage in the process — it’s woven through every layer of it. Which is exactly why you can’t hand it to AI and expect it to work.

## No One Ever Downloaded Fifteen Years of Pattern Recognition

Press enter or click to view image in full size

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*o2oHcWHEKei2g_CdHNYZmA.png)

There’s a reason Airbnb brought Chip Conley — who’d built and sold Joie de Vivre, the second-largest boutique hotel brand in America — in as head of hospitality. Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia were brilliant founders, but Conley had been around the block more times than they had.

**No amount of reading gives you what decades of reps gives you.** That’s not an opinion — it’s why the McKinsey interview process is brutal. Those people are smart because they earned it, not because they read a playbook about being smart.

**This is the real problem with prompt playbooks. They’re selling you the output of someone else’s judgment without the judgment itself.** Here’s a McKinsey consultant prompt — great, now you have words that sound like a McKinsey consultant. But you don’t have the years of structured thinking, the pattern recognition across thousands of problems, the ability to smell bullshit in a slide deck at thirty paces. You just have words.

Judgment comes from reps, intentional exposure to ideas across domains, being a genuine student of whatever you’re doing, caring enough to ask hard questions and the ability to sit with uncomfortable answers.

I spent fifteen years interviewing over a thousand people on a podcast. Every episode was a series of judgment calls —

-   Is this person worth interviewing,
-   Is this line of questioning working.
-   Is the audience going to fall asleep in the next thirty seconds.

That’s where my ability to work effectively with AI comes from fifteen years of learning how to extract real thinking from people through questions. You can’t compress that. But you can adopt the posture that makes it possible — staying curious, reading across domains, never assuming you already know the answer. That’s the foundation.

## Judgment Isn’t Subjective — It’s Uncalibrated

Press enter or click to view image in full size

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*81ULsGvPtwr_FnswPo5NvA.png)

Most people treat judgment like it’s some mystical quality — you either have good instincts or you don’t. That’s wrong. Judgment is data you haven’t bothered to track.

Every decision you make produces an outcome. Every outcome is a data point. But most people never close the loop. They make a call, it goes sideways, they move on and make the same call again six months later because they never encoded what they learned.

A famous author’s assistant once pitched him for our podcast and immediately started negotiating down the time. She told me he’d provide more value in thirty minutes than all our other guests had in an hour — obvious bullshit from someone who’d never listened to the show. We compromised on forty-five. He was average at best.

After a few rounds of this with different guests, we encoded a policy: every interview is an hour. Oprah, the Dalai Lama, doesn’t matter — give us the hour or we pass. Fame as a selection criteria got eliminated because a few bad calls gave us the data to see it was a broken proxy for quality.

Elite consulting firms do the same thing with hiring — recruit from fifteen schools, filter on GPA, run structured cases. It looks elitist, but what they’ve actually done is systematize judgment for consistent data collection. The criteria are explicit, applied the same way every time, and updated based on outcomes. That’s not intuition — that’s calibration.

Don Moore puts it this way in _Perfectly Confident_: the problem with intuition is that it results from unconscious processes, which means you can’t audit it. If your judgment stays in your head, you’re always at the mercy of whatever emotion you’re feeling that day. Emotions aren’t the enemy — they have to be part of the equation. But without some level of encoding, emotions hijack the decision instead of informing it.

With AI, the encoding looks different. You’re not writing criteria in a spreadsheet. You’re learning a posture — interrogating the output instead of accepting it. Here’s something most people don’t realize: every answer you get from a language model about how to do something isn’t the best approach. It’s the most commonly documented approach, which is whatever survived the training data.

Ask how to start a business, you get the MBA playbook. And ask how to get healthy, you get the same generic advice that’s been written ten thousand times. Not because it’s right for you — because it’s what exists in the most volume. You push back: strip the assumptions, assume the first answer is wrong, and ask what it actually takes. That’s judgment applied in real time — dissolving the assumptions baked into the response and forcing a rebuild from what’s actually true.

The difference between someone with calibrated judgment and someone without it isn’t intelligence. It’s whether they’ve been paying attention to their own decisions long enough to know when something smells wrong — and whether they’ve encoded enough of that into a repeatable process that they’re not reinventing the wheel every time they sit down.

## Your Media Diet Is Training Data for Your Brain

Press enter or click to view image in full size

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*h1yRbV2ca_4uEOmjrX7YqQ.png)

Your media consumption is an environment. The books you read, the podcasts you listen to, the feeds you scroll — Jim Bunch calls this your mimetic environment, and it shapes your thinking whether you’re conscious of it or not.

Most people’s mimetic environment is garbage. Algorithmic feeds designed to maximize engagement, not understanding. Shallow takes optimized for shares. Content that confirms what you already believe. You can’t develop good judgment in that environment any more than you can get strong by sitting on the couch.

Ryan Holiday reads across domains so aggressively that a book about Stoicism ends up pulling from boxing, baseball, military history, and business in the same chapter. That’s not a research strategy — that’s fifteen years of intake structured to produce unexpected connections. He can see connections that other people miss because he’s been deliberately building the pattern library that makes those connections possible.

The parallel to AI collaboration is direct. **Most people approach AI with the same shallow consumption mindset they bring to their Twitter feed**. They take the output at face value, don’t question it, don’t probe it, don’t push back. They get what the algorithm serves them and call it a result.

The people who get real value treat every interaction as a thinking tool. They know that AI output is trained data articulated as authority — it sounds confident because it’s statistically weighted toward confident-sounding text, not because it’s correct. Your lived experience articulated clearly is worth ten times that. But only if you have the judgment to know the difference.

### The Difference Between Skepticism and Stubbornness Is Knowledge

Here’s what happens when most people ask AI how to do something hard: AI pushes back. It tells you it’s a massive undertaking, lists all the reasons it’s complex, and nudges you toward the safe, bloated approach. Most people accept that at face value. The ones with judgment say — okay, now strip every assumption you just made and tell me what it actually takes.

That’s not stubbornness. That’s skepticism applied to AI output — and it’s one of the most underrated components of judgment in this context.

AI defaults to the most commonly documented approach, not the best approach. It defaults to complexity because complexity is what’s in the training data. Enterprise engineers build Kubernetes clusters because that’s what enterprise engineering looks like in text form. But sometimes flat JSON files and file-based coordination solve the same problem more reliably than distributed systems — if you understand what behavior you actually need and work backwards to the structure that produces it.

That insight — structure produces behavior — is the lens that lets you call bullshit on AI suggestions. Not just “this seems hard,” but “this structure doesn’t produce the behavior we need.” Most people don’t have that frame. So they either accept whatever the AI produces or they reject it out of frustration without knowing why. Neither is useful.

**Skepticism with knowledge is judgment. Skepticism without knowledge is just stubbornness.** The difference is understanding what you’re actually trying to accomplish at a systems level, so you can evaluate whether the proposed solution actually gets you there.

The bottleneck was never the model. It was always the person using it.

### Three Questions to Ask Yourself

Before accepting any AI output, ask:

1.  What assumptions is this response making that I haven’t validated?
2.  Is this the best approach or just the most commonly documented approach?
3.  Would I bet money on this being correct?

If you can’t answer these confidently, you’re outsourcing your judgment.
